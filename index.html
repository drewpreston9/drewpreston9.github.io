Crime in Chicago

A look at trends in crime rates over the last decade
Created by Drew Preston

Crime in America’s largest cities was one of the focal points of President-elect Donald Trump’s campaign over the last year and a half. Cities like Chicago and Baltimore were often used as proof that things were getting worse.

Were things really getting worse, though? Was crime on the rise? Americans seem to be more worried about it.

The City of Chicago releases massive data sets every year with the police department's crime logs. Every incident, from a homicide to trespassing, is submitted to one central database. With this information, it’s easy to see the direction crime in the city is going.

Let’s start with what things look like this year. Here are the top five most common crimes in the city, with the number of police responses to each on the left.

These figures are police responses to calls; not all of these ended in arrests. These figures reflect 2016 through the end of October.

Homicides are often highlighted in the context of Chicago crime, but they never come close to cracking the top five most common types of police calls. There have been 685 homicides in Chicago so far this year, as of Nov. 27.

In any case, where are all these crimes taking place this year?

This is a map of the nine wards with the highest total crime. As of Oct. 30, they’d all crossed 6,000 police calls this year.

Now it’s time to look into how things have changed in the last decade. The total number of crimes decreased 45 percent in the last decade, even though the city’s population only decreased about 7 percent (based on the difference in 2000 and 2010 census counts).

Of course, this lumps lots of different trends together–it’s worth taking this data apart. Homicides have remained largely the same over the last decade–getting worse this year (data updated Nov. 27).

Still, the three most common crimes this year–theft, battery and criminal damage–have all followed the larger trend over the last decade.

At least in Chicago, certain crimes have gotten worse in recent years. But, the overall trend points in the opposite direction.
